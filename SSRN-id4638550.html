Decomposing Machine-Learning Alpha


Alessandro Laurent and Alexandre Deruaz* Unigestion SA


November 171 2023


Abstract

In recent years we observed the development of numerous machine- learning models used to predict future cross-sectional returns. The evidences presented suggest that non-linear models such as neural net- works and random forests have the ability to capture complex and time-varying relationships between stock characteristics and future re- turns, and indeed, these models describe cross-sectional returns with surprising accuracy. In this study we propose a general methodology to decompose the performance without prior knowledge of these mod- els details. We decompose the performance into four elements: the structural exposure to traditional factors, superior feature selection capability, their potential to dynamically adjust there prediction, and finally their ability to incorporate non-linearities and joint effects. We show that the surprisingly good performance of generic neural-network or random forest models trained on fundamental stock characteristics to predict future returns can almost entirely be traced back to a linear combination of factors, with limited and mostly not statistically sig- nificant alpha assigned to time varying relationships, non-linearities or joint effects. This is particularly true for smaller investment universes such as Europe or the US where dimensionality might not be suffi- ciently large to fully exploit the potentials of these complex non-linear models. The evidence suggests that the competitive advantage of these models compared with traditional linear ones is limited, but may be a powerful tool to evidence attractive features explaining performance beyond established factors.

  1. Introduction

    Over the last seventy years, since the development of the Capital Asset Pric- ing Model (CAPM), the majority of the related literature has been devoted to the search of pricing anomalies, or factors, that could explain and forecast the cross-sectional returns of securities. While earliest criticism of the CAPM can be traced to the 1970 s (see for example Haugen and Heins (1972) and Haugen and Baker (1996) on the low risk anomaly), the literature evolved to the point that Harvey, Liu, and Zhu (2016) examined 296 published and claimed significant factors while Hou, Xue, and Zhang (2020) increased the number of potential predictors to 452. While few of these factors - namely the one proposed by Fama and French (1992) and Carhart (1997): Size, Book-to-Price and Momentum (FFC-factors) - became well established and accepted by researchers, the others needed to pass the "alpha significance test", i.e. the historical performance needed to be significant (have a positive and significant intercept) controlling for the FFC factors, to be recognised as true price anomalies. On top of FFC factors characterising Value, Size and Momentum risk premia, in the recent years Novy-Marx (2013) study on Profitability (NM-factor), and Frazzini and Pedersen (2014) results on the Low Beta anomaly (FP-factor) established themselves as recognised charac- terisation of respectively, Quality and Low Risk risk premia. We will refer generically to these five established factors as the common factors.

    During the last decade, the asset pricing literature resorted to new tools borrowed from computer science, that brought the search for predictive fac- tors to a new level: non linear machine-learning models. Because the number of return predictors is potentially very large and the interaction among them possibly non linear, machine-learning offers a natural way to accommodate a high-dimensional feature set and flexible functional forms to uncover complex patterns and hidden relationships. The research shifted therefore from the la- borious identification of incremental pricing anomalies under strict statistical criteria and their integration into parsimonious linear models, to the devel- opment of fully unrestricted and unstructured time-varying models, using extensive lists of predictors combined in complex, non-linear ways. Theoret- ically, this increase in flexibility leads to better forecasting and a more pre- cise description of future cross-sectional returns. In practice, crude machine- learning models deliver surprisingly good results. However, this comes at the cost of vanishing economic interpretability, and leaves researchers exposed to the risk at losing both economic and statistical validation of the results. A lot of work remains indeed to be done, first, to understand what are the elements of machine-learning processes that are associated to improved fore- casting performance, and second, to assess if the reduction in interpretability is justified by the existence of a significant non-linear and time-varying rela- tionship between predictors and a consequent increase in predictability not achievable by linear models.


    2


    We contribute to this question proposing a general methodology to de- compose the forecast performance of a machine-learning algorithm, indepen- dent of the type of model used. In particular we decompose the performance into four components: (1) the structural exposure to Fama-French, Carhart, Novy-Marx and Frazzini-Petersen common factors, (2) a better selection of additional features within each risk premia, beyond common factors, or the ability to identify, among the large number of potential features, the ones that have a significant predictive power over the full period under analysis,

    (3) the time-varying component, i.e. the ability of the algorithm to adapt the model exposure to the different predictors dynamically at each calibration, increasing the importance of the ones for which the predictive power has increased and reducing the ones for which it has deteriorated, (4) the non- linearity component, which captures all additional non-trivial interactions across predictors.

    Starting from a machine-learning output signal identified to an expected return, hence without prior knowledge of the model, we run for each period a cross-sectional regression akin to Fama and MacBeth (1973) but controlling for market beta impact. Doing so, we obtain the return of the machine- learning signal defined as the time-series of the regression coefficients. In parallel, we do the same jointly for the common factors. The first step of the analysis consists in regressing the machine-learning returns on the returns of the common factors to assess the significance of the intercept, interpreted as the excess performance unexplained by structural exposure to common factors. This is the analogous "alpha significance test" previously mentioned, used to assess the existence and relevance of a potential price anomaly. The second step of our methodology consists in assessing the feature selection power of machine-learning. Using the residuals of the previous regression we investigate the existence of any features in the predictor set used to train the machine-learning algorithm that can explain in a static and linear way a proportion of performance missed by the traditional common factors.

    Should the machine-learning performance still be statistically positive after controlling for the additional factors, we then test the time-varying and the non-linear component. To assess the ability to optimally change the factor exposure over time, we split the training period into sub-periods and run multiple factor performance attributions to assess the stability of the excess performance and the different factor coefficients. Finally, we look at the relations between the predictor and the machine-learning signal to get an indication of the potential non-linearity in the relations.

    Consistent with the results in Avramov, Cheng, and Metzker (2022) we find that in liquid markets accessible to institutional investors, the perfor- mance of machine-learning signal is almost entirely explained by a static linear combination of factors. We can therefore reject for most of the cases in our analysis the necessity of a non-linear model, as the performance would not justify the lack of interpretability of the signal. We would therefore use

    the machine-learning algorithm as a powerful and flexible tool for factor identification but then combine the different factors in a static and linear way to maintain the interpretability of the model and a better control of the factor exposures of the final portfolio.

    The rest of the paper is organised as follows. Section 2 describes the data and the machine-learning models used in our analysis. Section 3 presents the methodology used to decompose the machine-learning predictability. Section 4 investigates the empirical results and implications. Section 5 concludes.

For both specifications we use the adaptive moment estimation algorithm (Adam) for the stochastic gradient descent used in the optimisation. The second type of models is random forest, considered in a very generic form as proposed in Pedregosa et al. (2011) with default parametersl.

  3. Methodology and Results in European Universe

    The preliminary step in our methodology consists in computing the realised return of the machine-learning strategy from its signal, i.e. the vector of expected returns, without knowledge of any details on the model itself. We estimate this realised return through a cross-sectional multiple regression, using the Fama and MacBeth (1973) methodology. Rather than regress- ing directly cross-sectional returns on the factor exposures though, we ad- just the returns by the stock market beta. We apply such adjustment as we are interested in the ability of the machine-learning factor to forecast the cross-sectional excess returns, independent of the market beta of the resulting strategy. This distinction is particularly important for low-beta or low-volatility anomalies, for which we need to disentangle the anomaly (low beta/volatility stocks tend to achieve higher expected returns3) from the overall beta exposure (a portfolio of low volatility stocks will have an overall lower beta, hence a potential (tail-) headwind in strong (down-) up- markets), which could be achieved simply by investing a β amount in the market portfolio and a (1-β) amount in a risk-free rate. As we observe that low volatility/low beta are important factors in describing the performance of machine-learning forecasters and as such include them as one of the fac- tors in the next steps, it facilitate the interpretation of the results to work directly with beta adjusted excess returns.

    To efficiently estimate factor excess returns, we follow Grinold and Kahn (2000) and run generalised least squares (GLS) regressions, weighting each observed stock return by the inverse of its specific variance. In the specific, following Menchero (2010), we weight each observation by the square root of its associated stock market capitalisation, with two benefits. First, estima- tion error is reduced, as the square root of its market capitalisation acts as a proxy for the inverse of its specific variance. Second, with such a weighting scheme and appropriately normalised factors, the regression intercept can be identified to the market-cap weighted market return. Finally, as a con- sequence, the time-series of the estimated regression coefficient bt can then be interpreted as the beta-adjusted excess return of the machine-learning factor4.

    The same methodology is applied jointly to the common factors. Fol- lowing Fama and French (1992), Carhart (1997), Novy-Marx (2013) and Frazzini and Pedersen (2014), we collect for each security over the test set the book value to price ratio (BtP), the log of the market capitalisation multiplied by -1 (SmallCap), the logarithm of the last month return minus the last year return (lnRet1201), the return-on-asset (RoA) and the ex-ante market beta also multiplied by -1 (LowBeta). Similar as before each factor is gaussian-normalised. We then compute the time-series of beta-adjusted excess returns for each of the different factors, resulting from the series of multivariate regression coefficients at each point in time.

    2. Second Step: Feature Selection

      Part of the residual excess return after step one of our methodology (i.e.the regression s intercept) could be attributed to the ability of the machine- learning model to better identify, beyond the recognised common factors, additional features or anomalies that capture the cross-sectional stock re- turns. To address this question, in step two we regress the residuals of pre- vious time-series regression on the excess returns of the different predictors used to train the machine-learning algorithm. The excess returns of each predictor are estimated with the same adjusted Fama and MacBeth (1973) methodology used in step one. We group the predictors by risk premia to understand if machine-learning can help achieving a better description of the different widely recognised categories. Each factor is therefore linked to one of the following risk premia: Low Risk, Value, Quality and Momentum. The aim is to understand if we can add additional predictive features the common factors used in step 1 or even replace one of the factors with a better one.

      For each risk-premium we therefore regress the residual obtained in the previous step on the excess return of the different predictors used to build the machine-learning factor. We then select the predictor with the most significant coefficient, and test if among the remaining ones, there are oth- ers which have additional explanatory power (i.e. a coefficient significantly different than zero, both alone and in the presence of the previously selected predictor). If we focus on value for instance (Table 4 reports all the regres- sion coefficients) the only factor that is statistically significant in describing the residual returns is free cash-flow to enterprise value. Other significant predictors are - in the case of NN2-10J for Europe - the max total return over the previous month (MaxRet1M) for Momentum, which captures the short-

    3. Third Step: Time-varying Effect

      Having assessed the ability of a machine-learning model to identify the set of risk factors capable to describe future cross-sectional excess returns, the next step is to analyse the existence of some timing property and the poten- tial for the machine-learning model to optimally vary the exposure to these factors over time according to changes in the market environment. To assess this ability we decompose the sample into sub-samples of equal length and compute for each subset the exposure of the machine-learning signal excess returns to the excess returns of the set of factors identified in the previous step. Figure 3 illustrates the resulting regression coefficients for each of the factors. We compare the coefficients for five subsets with the static regres- sion coefficient obtained over the full sample. First, while the sub-sample regression coefficients do vary over time, they are hardly statistically dif- ferent from static regression model coefficients. We take this as evidence that the factor exposures are essentially structural, and that little dynamic behaviour is at play. Second, as we try to assess the ability of the machine- learning model to optimally time the different factors identified as significant over the full testing period, we would expect the intercept (i.e. the excess performance unexplained by structural exposure to common and additional factors) of the rolling regressions to be on average lower than for the full sam- ple. This does not seem to be the case for the model under consideration. We therefore conclude that in this particular case the time-varying compo- nent is negligible, time-varying factor exposures only explain a marginal and not statistically significant part of the machine-learning alpha. In fact, for the specific European NN2-10J model under review, the average excess per- formance unexplained by the rolling estimation, as reported in Table 6 is only 1 basis point lower than in the case of the structural model. Only 1 basis point a month is thus attributable to dynamic features of the model. We show similar results in appendix B.3 for models with shorter estimation window.

    4. Fourth Step: Non-linearities and Joint Effects

      Finally, the part of the machine-learning performance that is neither ex- plained by factor identification nor by time-varying factor exposures could be linked to non linear relations across the different factors optimally cap- tured by the machine-learning algorithm. Identifying such relations and the ability to capture them is however not a straightforward task.

      To illustrate potential non-linear and joint effects, we follow a similar methodology suggested by Noguer i Alonzo and Zoonekynd (2022) and we plot the relation between the machine-learning factor scores and the scores for each of the factors identified in step two, using hexagon bins as well as median-per-quantile portfolios as reported in 4.

      While some vague non-linearity could potentially be observed, in par- ticular in lnRet1201 or EquityGrowth, the relations between the machine- learning excess returns and the different factor excess returns appear es- sentially linear. There is no strong evidence suggesting that the machine- learning model is capturing some non-linearity in the expected returns as a function of input features, at least not when the analysis is conducted on the set of factors identified as significant in step 2 of our methodology. For the same reason we conclude that there is also no joint effect across factors to expect. Potentially, the machine-learning factor could incorporate some non-linearity in other factors, not significant over the full period, and there- fore not included in our analysis. The idea, however, that some non-linear combination of not-significant factor could potentially be significant over the full sample, while possible is probably quite unlikely, in particular given that the unexplained excess performance, i.e. once a linear factor combination is taken in consideration, is not significant.

      Finally, it has to be noted that to our knowledge, there is no proper methodology to test the presence of non-linearity and to infer the func- tional form and the predictors involved. We can therefore only somehow illustrate the importance of non-linearity in the assessment of a machine- learning model. So far the elements collected in our analysis, however, show a very modest importance.

  5. Conclusion

Machine-learning models applied to financial datasets seem to deliver im- pressive results in explaining the cross-section of equity returns, notably when compared to more traditional approaches such as factor-based stock selection method. Similarly to many other fields of application, the power of machine-learning models is however balanced with the difficulty in explain- ing the results, and the potential risk of over-fitting. These problems are even more pronounced as these usually proprietary models inner details are not shared with potential users and investors.

To overcome these issues, we proposed a methodology to analyse machine- learning alpha without prior knowledge of their implementation. The ap- proach helps measuring the true value added of these models, and clearly at- tribute their benefits to different features usually expected from such model: exposure to favourable risk premia, superior feature selection, dynamic ad- justment to evolving market conditions and the ability to exploit non-linear effects. The different steps of methodology can be summarised as follow:

  1. Attribute the structural exposure of the machine-learning score to the recognised Fama-French-Carhart (FFC) factor alphas by regress- ing machine learning excess returns on the standardized FFC factor performance,

  2. Consider additional financial features able to explain any residual al- pha on top of usually recognised FFC factors, through the selection of statistically significant factors to explain the residuals of the initial regression,

  3. Verify the ability for the machine-learning model to favourably adjust it s factor exposures through time, considering the reduction in unex- plained performance over shorter time periods when regressing excess returns,

  4. Consider non-linearities in the distribution of retained features through the distribution on the machine-learning score.

Considering standard machine-learning models, we show that, while per- formance seems initially impressive for such simplistic models, the results are essentially explained by structural exposure to well-known and long de- scribed risk premia. We confirm the ability of these models to perform additional feature selection, as in most cases, unexplained performance is no longer statistically significant once recognising exposures to few additional features.Finally, we conclude that there is a potential benefit in the time- variation of machine-learning parameters, but only if the universe is hetero- geneous enough and we were unable to evidence any non-linearity benefit.


25


We conclude that simple machine-learnings models performance is essen- tially explained by structural and to some extent dynamic exposures to well documented features, and investors should be careful in attributing the im- pressive results to unique features of machine learning model such as ability to deal with high dimensionality of data and non-linear effects.

We see two interesting directions of research to complement our results. The first is developing better statistics helping judging the non-linear effect reflected by machine-learnings models, as this key potential benefit of such complex methods is hardly evidenced in financial applications so far. Second, we would welcome the development of more structured models going beyond simple feed-forward neural networks or tree-based models, similarly to the most recent developments in other fields of application.
